UX/UI Design project: CareerSync

{# row 1 #}

What is CareerSync?

CareerSync is a concept platform designed to satisfy CSCI 443 Course project requirements. The development process included following a series of UX Design stages as our wonderful professor, Dr. Laura Stanely, provided lectures and supplementary information throughout the duration of the semester. CareerSync is meant to be a platform in which users can manage their job hunting materials and utilize tools to make the process of finding a job more efficient.

Project's Adobe Spark page
{# row 2 #}
The Problem

As the world begins to ease its way back to a new normal, millions of Americans are quitting their jobs in search for new opportunities. At the same time, millions of people who graduated in the first years of the Covid-19 pandemic and soon to be college graduates are begging to look to start their careers. With thousands of different ways to find and apply for jobs, it's impossible for individuals to organize and track their job search activities. Pandemic aside, on average it takes between 100 and 200 job applications to get just one job offer! If successful, this web platform will allow individuals who are looking to gain employment and organizations such as career coaching companies to monitor application status's, set reminders for follow-ups and interviews, and view statistics about their job search that will allow them to make better decisions in their hunt for employment.

Tools used

During this project, I was able to utilize a really awesome set of tools and pieces of software that I thought were genuinely really impressive. Most of these were recommended by Dr. Laura Stanely specifically throughout the development process, some I sourced myself. If the tools required additional lecturing and explanation, there was an accompanying demonstration video produced by the courses Teaching Assistant, Buwani Manuweera.

The first and most essential was Adobe Spark, where I developed my report for each of the six phases of the design process. Next during the Empathize Stage, I used Google Forms in addition to Zoom to complete information gathering tasks. With this information I needed to fill out empathy maps for each of the surveyed individuals using the provided templates. For this I used Miro, which allows manipulation of PDF documents like no other service I've found.

During the ideation stage, I used Coggle to complete a mind mapping process. During the prototyping stage I used Adobe Color to concept board color schemes for the CareerSync platform prototype. I used Mockitt to create a high fidelity prototype, and classic pen and paper to create my low-fidelity prototype sketches. During the testing phase I again used my high fidelity prototype along with a timer, and an assistant to gather usability heuristics data.

{# row 3 #}
Getting to know the users

The objective of this stage was to get to know a set of users and try to understand who they are, and what their needs and problems are. For this, I interviewed a relatively diverse set of people that were all actively searching for a new source of employment or were recently hired for their current position. For each of these users using a survey and additional expanding conversations the following topics were addressed:

  • Age
  • Gender
  • Highest level of education
  • Current employment status
  • Years of professional work experience
  • What industry or sector they work in or plan to work in
  • How they found their current or most recent employment position
  • Amount of applications submitted during last job search, and the outcome of this search
  • The amount of unique industries in their last round of job applications
  • Preferred method of communication with recruiters/hiring managers
  • The largest challenge of hte job application process not including gaining employment
  • How they keep organized during a job search
  • What their resume/cover letter/portfolio formatting usually looks like
  • How they keep track of which jobs they have applied for

During this stage a single persona was created to help personify the most common set of user characteristics and features found during the survey. This was used alongside other tools such as a Business Model Canvas to refine and define what parts of our users information would be most important during the rest of the design process.

{# row 4 #}
Finding structure in the data

Now that I had this overwhelming surplus of information about my users and their needs it was time to distill the data into some possible solutions. This was done using a set of creativity techniques such as mind-mapping and simple general brainstorming. Mind-mapping really helped to solidify what features what be required to provide a solution, and how they would interact with one-another.

{# row 5 #}
Putting this data to work

Finally it was time to build something. I knew that because of my lack of design experience I would need to produce a large quantity of possible prototypes. Therefore, after creating a User Flow diagram to solidify how users might navigate the functionalities of the site I began sketchy dozens of low fidelity prototypes. This was an activity that confirmed the idea that I should never pursue a career as an artist. However, after discarding the more obviously bad designs I found myself with a set of views that could really define a decent site. With these views I used Mockitt to create an interactive prototype, along with colors and inspirations from a concept boarding activity concluding with what I would consider a rather impressive prototype.

Then, one by one, I went through each of the usability heuristics and UX laws and made sure that my site abode by each one. This somewhat simple process ended up having a profound impact on the final prototype. This is a process that I will always include in my projects whether they be UX/UI Design or not.

{# row 6 #}
Does this thing work?

For my final stage I needed to verify that this thing actually worked. Or more specifically, what parts of this prototype were proficiently 'usable'. For this I created a list of tasks for users to complete using my interactive prototype. Tasks such as registering and navigating to a home page, or using a feature starting from the home page. Each of the tasks was meant to measure how usable each of the functionalities were, and to verify that the prototype fit each of the previously mentioned usability heuristics and UX laws. I tracked the amount of time and clicks it took users to complete the tasks and how many pages the user needed to look before they found the task objective. Through this process I was able to discover that there were certain features such as documentation and or linking other career platforms to the user's profile that were taking significantly longer. If I were to continue development on this platform I would loop back to the ideation phase and try to come up with solutions to minimize these statistics.
